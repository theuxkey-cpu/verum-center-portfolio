Three Research Workstreams That Diagnosed Why a Tire Platform Was Losing Customers

Led UX research across three interconnected failures in PneuStore 360° — from behavioral data and NPS analysis to strategic design direction — covering O2O installation, vehicle-context search, and wrong-size purchases.

PneuStore 360° UX Research Behavioral Data Analysis O2O / Omnichannel B2C

Context

PneuStore 360° is Cantu Inc.'s direct-to-consumer e-commerce channel for automotive tires — part of the same distribution network that powers 6,000+ auto centers across Brazil. The platform allows customers to browse, purchase, and schedule tire installation at partner shops nationwide.

Despite significant commercial investment, three structural UX failures were driving abandonment, wrong purchases, and customer churn. I was brought in to investigate each one from discovery — using behavioral data, NPS qualitative analysis, and stakeholder walkthroughs — and define the design direction before solutions were built.

The three workstreams were independent in origin but shared a common root: the platform was designed around products, not around the complete tire-buying journey.

My Role

  • ○ Led end-to-end UX research for all three workstreams — behavioral data analysis (Microsoft Clarity), NPS/qualitative synthesis (SoluCX), stakeholder mapping, and benchmarking
  • ○ Produced structured research artifacts: root cause analysis, JTBD mapping, customer journey maps, prioritization matrices, and sprint roadmaps
  • ○ Defined the conceptual model for the "Compra e Instale" O2O flow and the vehicle-context fitment UX direction
  • ○ Identified that the wrong-size purchase failure was systemic — driven by 6 compounding root causes — and prioritized a sprint roadmap covering immediate, structural, and transformational interventions

Problem

Three failures, one underlying pattern: the platform treated the tire as the end goal. The customer's actual goal — getting the right tire installed on their car — was left for them to figure out.

Problem 01 — Compra e Instale

Buying a tire doesn't include getting it installed

The purchase flow ended at delivery. Installation — mandatory, complex, and anxiety-inducing — was left entirely to the customer post-checkout. The platform had partner shops, scheduling infrastructure, and installation capabilities. None of it was surfaced in the purchase experience.

Problem 02 — Página de Carros

Vehicle search returned results the user couldn't trust

Users searching by vehicle expected to see only compatible tires. The catalog returned a generic product list organized by brand, line, and size — with no vehicle context. The fitment engine already validated compatibility in the backend. The front-end never confirmed it, silently breaking trust at the most critical decision point.

Problem 03 — Erro de Medida

Users were buying the wrong tire size — and couldn't fix it

Customers were purchasing incompatible tire sizes at a frequency high enough to generate measurable NPS impact, operational cost, and churn. The interface offered no guided entry point by vehicle, no compatibility validation on the product page, and no friction before checkout. When the error was discovered post-delivery, the cancellation path was broken — turning a UX failure into a support and reputation crisis.

The NPS signal

Of 312 detractor comments with written feedback, 21 were direct reports of purchasing the wrong tire size — 6.7% of all detractors, with explicit verbatims describing how the interface led them to the error. An additional 99 comments contained keywords related to size, exchange, error, or return.

"The site induces error. I searched for a tire size, brand and model. In the same brand, the searched size appeared alongside other sizes. I bought the wrong one. The return after cancellation takes too long. No exchanges, only cancellations." — Detractor, NPS 7
"Before, there was a vehicle compatibility list on each product listing." — Promoter, NPS 10 — missing a feature that had been removed
"I was on the phone for an hour and a half. When the agent finally answered, the call dropped." — Detractor, NPS 0 — trying to correct a wrong-size purchase
The amplification pattern: Support was the largest category of detractor complaints (43%), above delayed delivery (33%). When users bought the wrong size, they tried to correct it — and couldn't reach anyone. The UX failure became a support failure, which became an NPS failure. The problems compounded.

Six root causes — not a single fixable issue

  • 1 The site guides by size, not by vehicle. No vehicle-first entry point on homepage or search. Users who don't know their size by heart have no guided path — they estimate, compare similar sizes, and error.
  • 2 Search is the highest-friction page on the site. 23,661 rage clicks on /search indicate filters don't work as expected and similar sizes appear without clear hierarchy.
  • 3 The PDP offers no compatibility validation. The vehicle compatibility list that previously existed on product pages was removed. No signal tells the user whether this specific tire fits their specific car.
  • 4 Mobile concentrates maximum risk. 82% of sessions happen on mobile — where sizes are harder to read, comparison requires excessive scroll, and the search field is prone to input error.
  • 5 The purchase flow has zero positive friction. No confirmation moment between PDP and checkout. The user can complete a purchase without a single validation of the selected size.
  • 6 Post-purchase has no exit. Once the error is discovered, self-service cancellation before shipping is impossible via the site. Users are forced to wait for delivery, then navigate a broken returns flow.

Solution Direction

01 — Compra e Instale: Installation as the default, not an afterthought

The central design decision: installation should be the pre-selected choice at checkout, not a secondary option the user has to discover. The logistics moment becomes a deliberate, visible decision point — not a hidden feature tucked in post-purchase.

Redesigned purchase flow:

PDP → Logistics choice (Install default) → Select partner shop → Schedule appointment → Checkout → Dual confirmation
Trust Signal

"Instalação Garantida" seal

Products eligible for the partner shop installation network carry a visible trust seal on the product card and detail page. It pre-answers the user's question before they have to ask: this tire is compatible with the installation service, a certified shop is available in your area, and the appointment is confirmed at checkout — not separately.

The seal functions as a persistent anchor across the funnel, not just at the logistics choice moment — reducing the cognitive load of evaluating whether installation is possible for a given product.

Figma — Compra e Instale: logistics choice + dual confirmation screen

02 — Página de Carros: vehicle context stays visible throughout

Once a user enters their vehicle, that context persists as a visible filter banner across the entire shopping session. Every product card shows a compatibility confirmation drawn from the backend validation that was already running. The catalog switches from product-first to vehicle-first — not by rebuilding the engine, but by surfacing what it already knew.

Before

Search "pneus para Onix" → generic catalog organized by brand/line/size → no vehicle context visible → user can't trust results → abandons or buys wrong

Direction

Vehicle selected once → persists across entire session → every result shows "Compatible with your Chevrolet Onix 2021" → incompatible products hidden or marked → user confidence restored

03 — Erro de Medida: a 3-sprint intervention roadmap

The wrong-size problem had 6 root causes and couldn't be solved with a single fix. The research produced a prioritized roadmap sequenced by impact versus effort — quick wins first, transformational changes last.

Sprint 1 — Quick wins: high impact, low effort
  • Confirmation modal before cart: "You're buying 205/55R16 — for which vehicle?" — positive friction that doesn't block experienced users
  • Visual hierarchy of tire size on PDP and listing — size becomes the most visible element, not the discount
  • Self-service cancellation before shipping — eliminates the post-error trap that currently destroys NPS
Sprint 2 — Structural: medium effort, high return
  • Compatibility checker on PDP — reintroduces a feature users explicitly noted as missing; confirms fit before add-to-cart
  • Search page overhaul — address the 23,661 rage clicks: autocomplete by size, filters that work, size-exact prioritization before similar alternatives
  • Size education inline — tooltip explaining what 205/55R16 means, at the moment the user is choosing
Sprint 3 — Transformational: vehicle-first entry
  • Vehicle-guided entry as the primary search pattern — plate, model/year, or "My Cars" for returning users — eliminates the root cause for users who don't know their size
  • Mobile-first redesign of the full purchase flow — addresses the 82% mobile traffic + 64% bounce gap

What I'd Do Differently

Cross-reference returns data from operations earlier

The NPS analysis surfaced the wrong-size problem quantitatively — but the NPS data only captured customers who responded to the survey. The actual scale of wrong-size purchases likely exceeded the 21 verbatims by a significant margin. A returns-data audit from the operations team (cancellation reason codes) would have sharpened the business case and accelerated prioritization. I'd push for that cross-reference at the start of the research, not after the behavioral analysis was complete.

Test the confirmation modal assumption before locking the sprint sequence

The modal ("You're buying 205/55R16 — for which vehicle?") is a strong hypothesis for preventing wrong-size purchases with minimal development effort. But positive friction can also reduce conversion if it feels intrusive for users who already know their size. I'd validate this with an A/B test before positioning it as Sprint 1 — the ordering of quick wins should be validated, not just prioritized by intuition.

Treat the three workstreams as one connected story earlier

The three research threads were initiated separately and converged in analysis. The shared root — platform organized around products, not around the complete tire-buying journey — became clear only after all three were mapped. Framing that unifying insight at the start of each investigation would have sharpened the research questions and made the recommendations more coherent from the beginning.
