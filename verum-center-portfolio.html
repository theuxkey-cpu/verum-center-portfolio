Product Design & Strategy · Cantu Inc.

Rebuilding an Enterprise Operational Console to Scale

Consolidated five fragmented internal tools into a single operational console used daily by support, finance, and operations teams across Brazil and international markets.

VerumCenter B2B · SaaS UX Strategy Information Architecture Mobile-First Enterprise

Context

Cantu Inc. is one of the largest automotive parts distributors in Brazil, with over 6,000 partner auto centers. The company was undergoing a strategic platform convergence — merging two separate commercial systems into a single unified global product: VerumSales Global (VSG).

The operational layer — the tool that support agents, finance analysts, and operations managers use to run day-to-day work — was called Conect. It had been patched and extended for years without a coherent information architecture, and was reaching its limits as the company scaled internationally.

I led the design and strategy for VerumCenter: a new operational console built to replace Conect, designed to serve the full complexity of the global platform from day one.

My Role

I worked as a hybrid UX + Product Strategy lead on this project. My responsibilities went beyond UI design:

  • ◎Led discovery sessions directly with the CEO and board to map platform requirements before a single screen was designed
  • ◎Translated platform-wide business rules into a structured knowledge system (Architecture Decision Records) that governed all design and engineering decisions
  • ◎Designed information architecture, interaction patterns, and key interfaces across the console
  • ◎Created the governance framework that allowed cross-functional teams to align on over 1,183 business rules without deadlock

Problem

Before VerumCenter, running a single customer operation required navigating five separate tools with no integration between them.

How work actually got done

  • ⚠
    WhatsApp + spreadsheets as ops layer. Sales reps and support agents coordinated order exceptions and escalations through WhatsApp groups and shared spreadsheets — no system of record.
  • ⚠
    Fiscal rules lived in people's heads. Tax classification decisions required phone calls to specialists. Rules changed per channel, per tax code, and per state — and there was no centralized reference.
  • ⚠
    The double-discount loop bug. A structural flaw in the pricing engine caused orders to be incorrectly priced when agents used a workaround field. Agents had learned to work around the bug — but the workaround introduced new inconsistencies downstream.

Systemic impact

Before

Supply-constrained orders took 20–30 days because no single view existed across systems. Credit limits for shared-ownership auto centers were invisible. Rule differences between countries lived in emails and institutional memory.

After

Single console with role-based views, consolidated order tracking, visible credit limits across economic groups, and a structured rule system accessible to any team without specialist intervention.

Research

Discovery ran in parallel with platform architecture design. Key inputs:

  • →Stakeholder interviews with support leads, finance managers, commercial directors, and operations coordinators — mapping informal workarounds that had become load-bearing habits
  • →Process mapping of the full order lifecycle: opportunity creation → fiscal classification → pricing → approval → fulfillment → post-sale
  • →Rule inventory: catalogued 1,183 business rules across 5 domains (identity, personas, calculation engines, gaps, intelligence)
  • →Seller feedback sessions on mobile: direct input from field sales reps surfaced the horizontal-scroll problem that drove a key design pivot

Solution

VerumCenter was designed around four principles: single source of truth, role-appropriate access, rule transparency, and global-ready from day one.

Iteration 01

Identity & Permissiveness Dashboard

The most complex design challenge: making 1,183 business rules legible to humans. Rules in VSG are multi-dimensional — a customer's permissions depend on scope, channel, tax regime, and country simultaneously.

I designed a filterable dashboard where any stakeholder can select Scope × Channel and immediately see which rules apply, with expandable cards showing rule rationale, dependencies, and known edge cases. It replaced phone calls to specialists with a self-serve interface usable by support agents and finance directors alike.

Iteration 02

Dynamic Country × Channel Filter Layer

As the platform expanded globally, a new problem emerged: rules that work in Brazil don't always apply in Argentina or Chile. Tax treatment, channel availability, and customer classification differ by country — and there was no shared tool to communicate what applied where.

I added a country dimension to the filter system. Business areas, legal, and country-specific commercial leads can now filter by country + channel to see a precise view of the rules that govern each market — without a separate documentation system per region.

Iteration 03

Mobile-First Card Layout

An early version of the seller-facing interface used data tables — the default pattern for dense operational data. Field testing revealed a fundamental problem: on mobile, horizontal scroll made the tables nearly unusable. Reps reported skipping screens entirely because navigating the table wasn't worth the effort.

The pivot: replaced tables with card-based layouts designed mobile-first. Each card surfaces critical data in a glanceable format, with progressive disclosure for details. Colored status badges (Phase Status + Order Status) became first-class elements of the card, visible without scrolling. Validated through seller feedback before shipping.

Governance System (ADR)

With 1,183 rules and multiple teams contributing requirements, alignment was the persistent bottleneck. I introduced an Architecture Decision Record (ADR) system — a structured format for logging decisions, the context behind them, alternatives considered, and consequences accepted.

ADRs gave teams a process for making decisions without escalating everything to leadership, and created an audit trail that prevented rule regressions as the platform evolved.

What I'd Do Differently

More seller testing earlier. The horizontal-scroll table problem was caught during field testing — but could have been caught in research if I'd prioritized mobile walkthroughs with reps earlier in the process. The pivot was fast once we had the signal; getting the signal sooner would have been better.

Separate the rule dashboard sooner. The Identity & Permissiveness Dashboard started as a feature inside VerumCenter and could have been its own standalone tool for business areas. Keeping it integrated was right for support agents — but business stakeholders (legal, finance, commercial) would have benefited from a dedicated, embeddable view earlier.
